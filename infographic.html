Study Strategies That Actually Work

Most of us study the same way we always have. But research in cognitive psychology says a lot of those habits are wasting our time. Here are the ones backed by real evidence.

A huge survey of college students found that 84% rely on re-reading their notes as their go-to study method. Re-reading feels productive because the material starts to look familiar, but familiarity is not the same as actually knowing it. That gap is why so many students are surprised on exam day.

5 strategies worth switching to

Ranked roughly by strength of research support.

1

Retrieval Practice

Put your notes away and try to recall the material from memory. Flashcards, practice tests, or just writing down what you remember on a blank sheet all count. The act of pulling information out of your brain is what strengthens it.

^ This one has the most research behind it.
2

Spaced Repetition

Study a little bit across several days instead of cramming everything the night before. Review after 1 day, then 3 days, then a week. It feels slower, but the material sticks for way longer because your brain has to work harder each time it retrieves it.

Pair this with #1 for the best results.
3

Interleaving

Mix up different problem types or topics in one session. If you're studying math, alternate between different kinds of problems instead of doing 20 of the same type. It's harder in the moment but trains you to recognize which approach to use, not just how to execute it.

4

Elaboration

Ask yourself "why does this work?" or "how does this connect to what I already know?" as you study. Generating explanations in your own words forces you to process the material at a deeper level instead of just reading it at face value.

5

Dual Coding

Combine words with visuals. Draw a diagram, sketch a timeline, or map out a concept. When you encode something as both a verbal idea and a visual one, you're creating two separate retrieval paths to the same information.

How common techniques stack up

Based on Dunlosky et al. (2013), a meta-analysis of hundreds of learning studies.

Technique Rating Why
Practice testing High Active recall, exposes gaps
Spaced practice High Fights the forgetting curve
Elaborative interrogation Moderate Builds connections, but requires effort
Summarization Low-Moderate Only useful if you do it well
Highlighting Low Passive; creates false sense of knowing
Re-reading Low Minimal effort means minimal retention

Studying more is not the answer. Studying differently is. Swap re-reading for self-testing, spread your sessions out, and mix topics together. It will feel harder, and that's the point. The difficulty is the learning.
